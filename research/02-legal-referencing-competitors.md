# Referencing Competitors on the CodeFirstGames Marketing Site — Legal-Risk Memo (US)

**Purpose:** Practical, sourced guidance for a solo founder writing comparative marketing copy that names **Unity**, **"Unity MCP"** (open-source community MCP servers, e.g. `justinpbarnett/unity-mcp`, now stewarded by `CoplayDev/unity-mcp`), and **Coplay** (Coplay.dev / CoplayDev), and argues a code-first approach is better.

> **This is not legal advice.** I am not a lawyer. This is research to help you make informed choices and to know where the sharp edges are. US law only; state laws vary and cases turn on specific wording. For anything high-stakes — a claim you're unsure you can prove, a cease-and-desist letter, or using a logo — **consult a trademark/advertising attorney before publishing.** Comparative advertising is repeatedly described by practitioners as *one of the riskiest forms of advertising* precisely because a single inaccurate factual claim creates direct liability. Treat that as the operating assumption.

## 1. Nominative fair use (US) — you MAY name a competitor to refer to their product

**The doctrine.** US trademark law lets you use someone else's trademark to *refer to that person's actual product* — including in comparative advertising — without it being infringement. This is "nominative fair use," and the leading framework is the Ninth Circuit's **New Kids on the Block v. News America Publishing, Inc., 971 F.2d 302 (9th Cir. 1992).**

**The three-part New Kids test.** A use qualifies as nominative fair use when:

1. **Necessity** — the product/service is *not readily identifiable without using the mark* (you genuinely need the name to talk about it);
2. **Minimal use** — you use *only as much of the mark as is reasonably necessary* to identify it (the word name, not their stylized logo/trade dress, unless truly needed);
3. **No implied sponsorship/endorsement** — you *do nothing that suggests sponsorship or endorsement* by the trademark owner.

**Boundaries and nuances:**
- The defense fails the moment your use *implies affiliation, sponsorship, or endorsement* by the competitor. That's the line you protect with plain text, accurate depiction, and (optionally) a disclaimer.
- Comparative advertising gets *extra* latitude: courts have long tolerated competitors using each other's marks to claim superiority (the Coke/Pepsi "cola wars" are the textbook example). The Fifth Circuit has applied a relaxed version — in direct comparative advertising you need to satisfy only *two* of the three New Kids factors.
- Nominative fair use is an **affirmative defense**: it protects a *truthful, non-confusing* reference. It does **not** protect a *false* factual claim — that's governed by false-advertising law (Section 2 below). Fair use answers "may I say their name?"; it does not answer "is what I said about them true?"

## 2. Comparative advertising law (US) — naming a competitor and claiming you're better is legal *if* it's true, substantiated, and not misleading

**Bottom line:** Truthful comparative advertising is lawful and is *not* prohibited by the Lanham Act. The FTC actively *encourages* truthful comparative advertising because it helps consumers. You may name Coplay and Unity MCP and say your approach is better — **provided every factual comparison is true and you can prove it.**

**What makes it actionable — Lanham Act § 43(a) (15 U.S.C. § 1125(a)) false advertising.** A competitor can sue if you make **a false or misleading statement of fact** about their product (or yours). The classic elements a plaintiff must show:

1. a false or misleading statement of fact (about your product or theirs) in a commercial ad;
2. it deceived, or had the tendency to deceive, a substantial segment of the audience;
3. the deception is **material** — likely to influence a purchasing decision;
4. the goods/services are in interstate commerce (a website meets this);
5. the plaintiff has been or is likely to be injured.

**Two flavors of falsity — and why "literally true but misleading" still bites:**
- **Literally false** claims: a court will *presume* deception and materiality. The competitor doesn't need survey evidence. This is the danger zone — a flatly wrong factual comparison is the easiest case against you.
- **Literally true but misleading** claims: the competitor must show *extrinsic evidence* (e.g. a consumer survey) that the audience was actually misled. Higher bar for them — but still a real exposure, and you don't want to be litigating it.

**Substantiation — you must have proof *in hand before you publish*.** The FTC requires advertisers to possess a reasonable basis for objective/factual claims *before* the ad runs. For a comparative claim, that means: for every "we do X, they don't" or "we're faster/cheaper/safer," you can point to a documented, current, apples-to-apples basis. Don't publish the claim and hope you can back it up later.

**What must be true about *every* factual comparison claim:**
- **Accurate** — literally true.
- **Substantiated** — you hold evidence supporting it *now*, before publishing.
- **Current & fair** — compares like-with-like and reflects the competitor's product *as it currently is* (products change; a claim that was true last year can become false). Don't compare your newest version against their old one without saying so.
- **Not misleading by omission or implication** — the overall impression a reasonable reader takes away must also be true, not just the literal words.

**Also on the radar: trade libel / commercial disparagement** (state-law cousin) — covered in Section 5.

## 3. Trademark status of the specific names

### 3a. "Unity" — a strong, actively-enforced trademark of Unity Technologies

"Unity" is a strong registered mark. Unity Technologies publishes explicit **Trademark Guidelines** that bind third parties, and they matter to you in three separate places:

**(i) In your product name "CodeFirstGames":** You're fine here — you are *not* putting "Unity" in your product name. **Keep it that way.** Unity's guidelines specifically prohibit third parties from (a) using a **product name confusingly similar to a Unity product** or that could be construed as a Unity product/service, and (b) registering or using "Unity" (or confusingly similar marks) as part of **your business name, product name, social handle, or domain name.** So: never name a product/feature/domain "UnityBuilder," "Unity Code First," "unity-codefirst.com," etc.

**(ii) In body copy ("a Unity plugin," "works with Unity"):** This is classic **referential / nominative use and is permitted** — you need the word "Unity" to say what your tool is compatible with. Unity's guidelines require you to:
- **Use "Unity" as an adjective modifying a generic noun**, never as a noun or verb. Write *"a plugin for the Unity® Editor,"* *"compatible with Unity,"* — not *"a Unity"* as a standalone noun, and never *"Unity-ify your scene."*
- **Do not imply affiliation, sponsorship, endorsement, or approval** by Unity.
- **Do not use Unity's stylized logo, cube icon, or other Unity graphic symbols** without written permission (word "Unity" in your normal font = fine; their logo = not without permission).
- **Include a trademark/non-affiliation notice** in your materials. Unity's own prescribed form is essentially:
  > *"This website / these materials / [product or company name] is/are not sponsored by or affiliated with Unity Technologies or its affiliates. 'Unity' is a trademark or registered trademark of Unity Technologies or its affiliates in the U.S. and elsewhere."*

**(iii) Referencing "Unity MCP":** See 3b.

### 3b. "Unity MCP" — a descriptive/community term, not a distinct registered brand you must fear (but "Unity" inside it is still Unity's mark)

- **"MCP" = Model Context Protocol**, an **open standard** created by Anthropic (Nov 2024) and, as of Dec 2025, donated to the **Agentic AI Foundation under the Linux Foundation**, with OpenAI, Google, Microsoft, Block and others adopting it. It functions as an industry-generic technical term for the protocol — using "MCP" descriptively to mean "the Model Context Protocol" carries very low trademark risk.
- **"Unity MCP"** is not, in practice, a single proprietary brand — it's a *descriptive label* ("an MCP server for Unity") used across multiple community projects. The best-known are the open-source GitHub repos `justinpbarnett/unity-mcp` and `CoplayDev/unity-mcp` (Coplay took stewardship of the popular open-source Unity MCP). Because it's descriptive and community/open-source, there isn't a strong distinct "Unity MCP" brand to infringe.
- **The catch:** the word "Unity" *inside* "Unity MCP" is still Unity Technologies' mark. So when you reference it, treat it as a referential use: say something like *"open-source MCP servers for Unity (commonly called 'Unity MCP')"* rather than presenting "Unity MCP" as an official Unity product. Don't imply Unity Technologies endorses or publishes it (they don't).
- **Practical framing:** because "Unity MCP" refers to a *category of open-source projects* rather than one company, comparing against it is closer to comparing against an approach/category than against a single competitor — lower disparagement risk, *as long as* your factual claims about what those servers do are accurate. If you specifically target Coplay's `CoplayDev/unity-mcp` repo, that ties back to Coplay (3c).

### 3c. "Coplay" — treat it as a protected brand name (registered or not)

- **Coplay** is a real commercial brand: **Coplay.dev / CoplayDev**, a San Francisco company shipping a paid AI assistant for the Unity Editor (pre-seed funded; reportedly rebranding toward "Aura" for Unity + Unreal). I could **not confirm a specific USPTO registration** for "Coplay" in this research (a definitive answer needs a [USPTO TESS/TSDR search](https://tmsearch.uspto.gov/)). **It does not matter much for you:** a brand in active commercial use has at minimum **common-law trademark rights**, and the nominative-fair-use / false-advertising rules apply the same way whether or not it's federally registered.
- **Naming Coplay in a comparison is legally OK** under nominative fair use — you need the name to refer to their product. The risk isn't *saying "Coplay"*; the risk is **saying something false or misleading about Coplay's product**, which is exactly where trade libel and Lanham Act § 43(a) live (Sections 2 & 5). Because Coplay is a *single identifiable competitor*, any inaccurate factual knock is squarely attributable and is the highest-risk comparison on your page. Keep every Coplay-specific factual claim airtight and current (their product is actively changing/rebranding — re-verify before launch and periodically).

## 4. Logos vs. word names — **use plain text, not logos**

**Recommendation: reference competitors by their plain word-name in your normal site font. Do not reproduce Unity's or Coplay's logos.**

Why:
- Nominative fair use factor #2 says use *only as much of the mark as necessary*. A word name is almost always sufficient to identify a competitor; a logo/stylized mark is more than necessary and weakens your fair-use position.
- Reproducing a logo raises the odds a reader infers **affiliation, sponsorship, or endorsement** — the exact thing that breaks nominative fair use and invites an infringement claim. Practitioners specifically flag that service marks (Unity's and Coplay's marks cover services/software) carry *greater* reproduction risk than goods marks.
- Unity's guidelines **expressly prohibit** using Unity's stylized logo / cube icon / product icons without written permission. Using the word "Unity" in your body font is fine; using their logo is not.
- Logos also drag in copyright (many logos are separately copyrighted) and "tarnishment"/dilution theories if you place the competitor's mark in a negative light.

If you ever *do* feel a logo is necessary (generally: don't), the rules are — use the *exact, unaltered, current* logo, keep it visually separate from your own branding so no one thinks it's yours, and make clear it belongs to the competitor. But the clean, low-risk path for a solo founder is **text names only**.

## 5. Disparagement vs. protected opinion/puffery — phrase to stay safe

The dividing line: **a false statement of *fact* is actionable; an *opinion* or obvious *puffery* is not.** Trade libel / commercial disparagement (a state-law tort) requires a **false statement of fact** about the plaintiff's product, published, made with malice (knowledge of falsity or reckless disregard), causing quantifiable economic loss. Opinion and puffery fail the "statement of fact" element.

**Not actionable (safe zones):**
- **Puffery** — vague, non-specific, non-verifiable boasting no reasonable buyer treats as literal fact: *"the best way to build Unity scenes with AI,"* *"a dramatically smoother workflow."* Courts don't treat this as a factual claim.
- **Clearly-framed opinion** — subjective judgments signposted as belief: *"We think code is a better fit for AI-driven editing,"* *"In our view, a code-first approach scales better."* The framing ("we think / in our view / we believe") signals opinion. **But note:** you can't launder a false, provable factual assertion into "opinion" just by prefixing "we think" — if the statement implies undisclosed false facts, courts can still treat it as factual. Opinions about *your own design philosophy* are the safest ("we believe code-as-source-of-truth is the right model").

**Actionable (danger zone) — concrete, provable, and false statements about the competitor:**
- *"Coplay corrupts your scene files"* / *"Coplay can't do incremental sync"* / *"Coplay is 3× slower"* — specific, verifiable factual assertions about *their* product. If untrue (or unprovable), this is where liability lives. If you want to make a claim like this, it must be **true and substantiated** (Section 2), and ideally phrased about the *approach* rather than a smear.

**How to phrase comparisons safely:**
- **Compare *approaches/architectures*, not moral character.** *"MCP-driven editor automation works by an AI issuing live editor commands; CodeFirstGames instead treats your C# as the source of truth"* — a factual, verifiable, neutral architectural contrast is fine (and if you state a competitor's mechanism as fact, make sure that mechanism description is accurate).
- **Sell your upside rather than trashing them.** *"Because CodeFirstGames keeps everything in version-controlled code, you get X"* beats *"Coplay's approach loses your work."*
- **Use opinion framing for judgments** ("we believe," "in our experience," "we think") and **puffery for superlatives** ("the cleanest way to…").
- **Reserve hard factual claims** ("faster," "no domain reload," "deterministic") for things about **your own product** that you can prove — those are safe and persuasive, and they sidestep disparagement entirely because they're about you, not them.
- **Avoid**: absolute negative factual claims about the competitor, mocking/tarnishing tone paired with their mark, and any claim you can't currently document.

## 6. Practical do / don't list for site copy

### SAFE TO DO
- **Name Unity, Coplay, and "Unity MCP" in body text** to refer to those actual products/approaches (nominative fair use).
- **Say what you're compatible with:** *"a plugin for the Unity® Editor," "works with Unity."* Use "Unity" as an **adjective + generic noun.**
- **Make superiority/opinion claims about your own philosophy:** *"we believe a code-first, version-controlled approach is a better fit for AI,"* *"the cleanest way to keep scene and code in sync."* (Opinion + puffery.)
- **Make hard factual claims about *your own* product** that you can prove: *"no per-edit domain reload," "deterministic, diffable output," "everything lives in git."*
- **Contrast architectures factually and neutrally:** describe how MCP/editor-automation tools work vs. how code-as-source-of-truth works — *provided your description of their mechanism is accurate.*
- **Add a trademark disclaimer** (see below) and keep it visible in the footer.

### DON'T DO
- **Don't put "Unity" (or a confusingly similar term) in your product name, feature names, logo, social handles, or domain.** "CodeFirstGames" is clear of this — keep it clear.
- **Don't reproduce Unity's or Coplay's logos / stylized marks / icons.** Plain text names only.
- **Don't imply Unity, Coplay, or Anthropic endorses, sponsors, or is affiliated with you.**
- **Don't make specific negative factual claims about Coplay or Unity MCP you can't currently prove** (speed, data loss, "can't do X," bug claims). This is the single biggest liability. Coplay = a named single competitor = highest-attribution risk.
- **Don't dress a false factual claim as "opinion"** by prefixing "we think." Framing doesn't save a provably-false implied fact.
- **Don't let a claim go stale** — Coplay/Unity MCP are actively evolving (Coplay is rebranding toward "Aura"). Re-verify comparisons before launch and periodically after.

### MUST BE TRUE (for every factual comparison, before it publishes)
- **Literally accurate** — not just technically true but non-misleading in overall impression.
- **Substantiated now** — you hold documented evidence *before* the copy goes live (FTC prior-substantiation rule).
- **Apples-to-apples & current** — compares the competitor's product *as it exists today*, like-for-like with yours.
- **About mechanism, not malice** — safest when it's a verifiable architectural fact, not a character attack.

### Recommended one-line trademark disclaimer (footer)
> *"CodeFirstGames is not affiliated with, sponsored by, or endorsed by Unity Technologies, Coplay, or Anthropic. Unity is a trademark or registered trademark of Unity Technologies; Coplay is a trademark of its respective owner; all other trademarks are the property of their respective owners. Comparative statements reflect our own testing and opinion as of the date of publication."*

(A generic *"All trademarks are the property of their respective owners"* is the common minimum, but the fuller version above also does the **non-affiliation** work Unity's guidelines specifically ask for — which the generic line alone does not.)

### Should comparison claims carry visible substantiation?
**Yes — where practical, show your work.** A disclaimer does *not* substitute for having proof, but visibly grounding a factual comparison (e.g. *"based on our July 2026 testing of version X," a linked methodology/benchmark note, or "as documented in [competitor's] public docs"*) both strengthens truthfulness and signals good faith. Pure opinion/puffery needs no citation; **objective, verifiable comparative claims should be traceable to something you can produce if challenged.**

## Consult an attorney before you publish if…
- you want to make any **specific, negative, factual** claim about Coplay's or Unity MCP's performance/capabilities;
- you're tempted to use **any logo**;
- you receive a **cease-and-desist** or nastygram from Unity or Coplay;
- you want a **head-to-head benchmark/comparison table** (these are high-value but the highest-liability format — every cell is a factual claim).

A one-hour review with a trademark/advertising attorney of your final competitor-comparison copy is cheap insurance relative to a Lanham Act dispute.
